# How I Memorized Yaku

I am about to describe the memory palace I created to help me remember
all of the yaku. Memory palaces are unique to each individual, meaning
that I chose the images that had the strongest connection for me, not
necessarily anyone else. Details and help making your own memory palaces, along
with a complete list of yaku, scoring tables, and scoring guide will
come in a later section.

I started by dividing yaku into groups based on how many han they score,
expressed as two numbers (open - closed). For example, Chanta is worth 1
han open and 2 han closed, so it goes in the 1-2 group (along with San Shoku
Dojun and Itsu). Yaku that score the same open or closed still use the same
2-number system (Toi-toi is 2-2, for example). Yaku that can only be closed,
like Pinfu, are grouped with a zero in the open slot, like 0-1.

## The Palace

It is recommended that you use a place you are intimately familiar with for
your memory palace(s), but since I had some experience creating them already,
I chose a completely fictional location. It's a large, modern house, mostly
white stone and dark wood, with large floor-to-ceiling windows. I am (apparemtly)
in the process of moving in, because the rooms are barely decorated and there
are moving boxes everywhere (I didn't actually make these decisions, I just
imagined the house and my brain filled in the gaps 🤷). It is in the
mountains and there are some remnants of snow on the ground as I approach.

### The First Group

I open the large, black door and step in. There is a hallway in front of me, with
rooms on the left and right. Further down, I can see where the hallway opens up
to a sunken living room (I guess I'm in a split-level house?). To my left, on the wall, is a group of
cubbies, made of wood, like folks might put various sets of keys in. It's shaped
like this:

<img src="/cubby-squares.png" alt="A grid of wooden cubbies for sorting yaku by their open–closed han value" width="300" />

These hold all of the 0-x yaku, the ones that only count if the hand is closed.
In the first square (0-1) are:
* Closed Tsumo: a sumo wrestler wearing a business suit (a _clothed_ sumo)
* Pinfu: A ballpoint pen with arms and legs wearing a [karate gi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karate_gi).
It's a _Pen_ doing kung _fu_, so _Pinfu_.
* Iipeikou: The main character of [this game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ico_north_american_cover.jpg)
(which I loved as a child), Ico, and he's laying down in bed. He's _eepy Ico_, or Iipeikou

Hopefully you are seeinghow this works by now. Most importantly, that I am choosing images
that _I_ find funny, cute, or compelling in some way. When you make your own Memory Palace,
you should choose images that appeal to you. In the next square are the yaku that are worth 0-2:
* Chitoitsu: _7 pairs_ of pants stacked in a cube. Each is a different color, in Roy G. Biv order,
top to bottom. The rainbow helps my remember that there are 7 of them.

In the third square:
* Ryanpeikou: since it's just 2 Iipeikous, I just imagine two of the eepy Ico characters above,
laying next to each other. One is in color and the other is greyscale, but I don't know why.
My brain just decided this and I accepted it, which is a good practice when building
Memory Palaces.

In the fourth square, which is the detached one hanging down, scoring 0-5 there are:
* Renhou: Ren from the cartoon [Ren & Stimpy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren_and_Stimpy_(characters)),
wearing a dress. The images in a palace can be crude if they are memorable.
* Nagashi Mangan: A square board, like a chess board, except there are mahjong tiles
on the board instead of chess pieces.

### The Second Group

In the second group are all of the yaku that score 1 open or 1 closed, so 1-1. It's an unfurnished
bedroom (all of the non-yakuman rooms are) and it's to my left as I continue down the hall.

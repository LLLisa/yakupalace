# How to Memorize Anything

by Lisa Knox

I, like many of you, learned how to play mahjong pretty quickly, but struggled to
learn the scoring. It is, if we are honest, an absurd little bit of calculation.
Then I was recommended a book called _Moonwalking with Einstein_ by Joshua
Foer. In it, he describes mnemonic (memorization) techniques used by thousands of people (probably)
to quickly and accurately remember huge amounts of data. Using these techniques, I memorized
all of the yaku in a weekend, and how to calculate fu the following weekend. In this
article I will tell you how to do it, and the first step is a little primer on how memory works.

## How Memory Works

Writing and Mathematics have only existed for about 1% of humanity's time on Earth.
Most humans were illiterate less than 100 years ago. The human ability to memorize
words and numbers is actually very weak because just beginning to evolve. We memorize these things mostly by rote,
meaning you just repeat the thing enough times that your brain remembers it. It
takes a lot of effort, it's far from accurate, and on top of it all, it's boring. However,
our ability to memorize images, faces, and locations has been with us all through
our evolution, since before we were human, and is our strongest
memorization asset. Mnemonic techniques hijack this functionality to trick our brains
into remembering without the rote, without the effort.

Imagine it's 100,000 years ago and you live in a hunter-gatherer community, someplace warm. You've
never seen writing or math, money doesn't exist, and aside from getting enough food
and protecting your family, your days are largely carefree.\* You remember the important things:
who your family is, what the good food looks like, what the dangerous food looks like,
what the dangerous animals look like. You remember where to hunt, where to gather,
and if you've been away from home for several days hunting and gathering,
you remember how to get home. Your memory has evolved for millions of years to accomplish
these simple tasks. It is simple, does not require much effort, and is almost always accurate.
This is the foundational premise of the Memory Palace.

## The Memory Palace

The idea is simple and we'll go through it quickly: You imagine yourself moving through an environment
and imagine images representing what you want to remember at certain pre-designated locations (loci)
along the way.

For example, let's say you want to remember how to spell "Cat" (bear with me here):
imagine yourself walking down the street you grew up on (a place you are very familiar with is essential,
it lessens the cognative load of remembering your way around). At your first neighbor's house, there is a
20-foot-tall chocolate milkshake. It's bubbly, delicious and refreshing-looking, and you can feel the
chill coming off the glass (the more vivid the image is, the more senses you can attach to it, the
easier it is to remenber). The ChoColate shake represents the letter "C".

At the next neighbor's house,
you see Adam, like Adam and Eve Adam. He's tall, muscular, a bit sweaty, wearing naught but a fig leaf and looking ready
for some temptation (images that are sexual in nature are also much easier to remember).
He represents the letter "A".

At the third neighbor's house is Mr. T., except his body is an anthropomorphized letter T.
He stands there, mohawk and gold chains and all, holding a cup of tea in one hand and in the
other, a golf tee (the more funny/weird/absurd an image is, the easier it is to remember).
Also, he's wearing a T-shirt I guess. He represents the letter "T".

Now, the next time you find yourself struggling to remember how to spall "cat", you just close your
eyes and imagine yourself walking down the street you grew up on. It really is that simple.

## The Parlor Trick

Yes, I hear you, this sounds like a cheap parlor trick. That's what everyone thinks at first.
But it is a tried and true method for remembering things that is, in fact, thousands of years old
(probably). People compete in [memory events](https://www.usamemorychampionship.com/) using this technique,
form [communties](https://artofmemory.com/) around it and there is substantial evidence that it even
helps [prople with Alzheimer's disease](https://alz-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/epdf/10.1002/alz.037841).
It is a valid and valued method of retaining information, and we are going to use it to
memorize all of the intricacies of riichi mahjong scoring.

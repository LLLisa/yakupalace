# The Suji Secret

So first off, the concept of suji is not a secret. It is well-known and documented
everywhere. Let's go over the concepts of suji briefly, before discussing the secret itself.

## The Concepts

First, we memorize these three sets of numbers: 1-4-7, 2-5-8, 3-6-9. When trying to discard
tiles that are safe (because an opponent is, or is likely to be, tenpai), we look to these three
sets of numbers for guidance.

The idea is that because [ryanmen](https://riichi.wiki/Ryanmen) waits are so common, we
can use these sets of numbers (suji) to help us decide which tiles are safe(r). There are only
6 possible ryanmen waits in each suit, and since a player cannot win off a tile they have
discarded (the furiten rule), we can eliminate which ryanmen waits our opponent may be
waiting on.

For example, if an opponent has a 5p in their discards, we know that they are not waiting
with a 3p-4p or a 6p-7p, so the 2p and the 8p become safer. We say these tiles are suji to
the 5p, and the concept is called omote-suji. They are not actually safe because ryanmen
waits only make up about 50% of waits in practice, so we could say that the 2p or the 8p
will be safe about half the time (any statisticians reading this, just let it go, it’s fine).

If a player has an 8p in their discards instead, the 5p can be said to be half-suji to the 8p,
because they may be holding 3p-4p which will still accept the 5p. If they discard the 2p (or
if they are in riichi and another player discards and they do not call it), then the 5p can be considered full suji. This
concept is called naka-suji.

These two concepts fall under "basic suji".

There are several more sub-concepts that fall under the suji category. I will not go into
depth here because there are countless articles on the internet explaining each of these in
detail.

* Ura-Suji: If they have discarded the 2p, the 3p-6p are more dangerous.

* Matagi-Suji: If they have discarded the 5p, the 3p-6p and the 4p-7p are more dangerous.

* Aida-Yonken: Basically a double ura-suji: If they have discarded the 2p and the 7p, the 3p-
6p are more dangerous, because they are ura-suji to both.

## The Beginner's Mistake

It sometimes happens among beginners that they see a 1p in the opponent's discards
and think that because it's part of the 1-4-7 suji, that it makes the 7p safe. This concept
is referred to as "long suji" and it is more of a meme than anything. There is no
way for a tile at one end of a suji set to influence what happens at the other end.

## The Secret

If we group the two concepts that tell us which
tiles are safer (basic suji) in one group and the concepts which tell us which tiles are more
dangerous in another, we will find that the dangerous tiles and the
safer tiles are mutually exclusive. Therefore, you don't need to memorize the second group
of concepts at all. Just knowing which tiles are safer is a complete education, at least in
practical terms.

For example, they have a 5p in their discards, so ura-suji tells us the 1p-4p and 6p-9p are dangerous
and matagi-suji tells us that the 3p-6p and the 4p-7p are dangerous. What's left? The 2p and
the 8p, which we already know are safer because of omote/naka-suji. Say they have the 7p in their discards:
ura-suji tells us the 3p-6p is dangerous and matagi-suji tells us that the 5p-8p and the 6p-9p are dangerous.
What's left? The 4p, which we already know is half-suji.

So the big secret is this: If we know our basic suji, the other concepts are
redundant. If you can tell if a tile is suji or half-suji, you understand everything
you need to know from a practical perspective. The differences in danger demonstrated
in the other concepts are marginal, single-digit probability percentages, and therefore
have very little value compared to the massive value of the basic suji concepts.

So just learn the basic concepts and move on. The deeper strategies will always be there if you want to learn them,
but you aren't really missing anything if you never do.
